The Rainbow Angle

After light bends twice and reflects once inside a drop, it exits at a specific angle from the incoming sunlight. What angle do you see the rainbow?

45 degrees

Most people guess 45°. The actual angle is slightly less — and it is different for each color.

Discovery

The Hidden Formula

The rainbow is not a thing in the sky. It is a direction. Every droplet at 42° from your shadow sends red light back to your eye.

Sunlight → Water Drop → Reflect → 42° Back
180° − 138° = 42°

The light is bent so strongly that it turns almost back on itself. The 138° deviation leaves only 42° between your shadow and the red arc.

Explanation

How the Numbers Work

1

Snell's Law

Light bends when it enters water. The amount depends on the refractive index n ≈ 1.33. The formula is n₁ sin(θ₁) = n₂ sin(θ₂).

1.000 × sin(60°) = 1.331 × sin(θ₂)
2

Internal Reflection

The light hits the back of the drop and bounces. This mirror-like reflection turns the light back toward the sun side — but still inside the drop.

1 bounce
3

Second Bend

Exiting the drop, light bends again. The total turn is the deviation angle. For red light it is 138°. That means the rainbow appears 42° from your shadow.

138° deviation
4

Dispersion

Violet light bends more than red. Its deviation is 140°. So violet appears at 40° — 2° lower than red. This creates the color band.

Red 42° → Violet 40°
Applications

Where Else Is Refraction Hiding?

Bending light is not just a sky trick. It is the core of lenses, fiber optics, and the human eye.

👓

Eyeglasses

Lenses bend light before it reaches your eye. The curvature of the lens changes the angle to correct vision — pure geometry.

📷

Camera Lenses

Multiple glass elements bend light at calculated angles to focus an image onto a sensor. Chromatic aberration is the same dispersion effect.

💎

Prisms

A triangular prism splits white light into colors exactly like a raindrop, but without the internal reflection. The same angles apply.

🔬

Microscopes

High-power lenses use oil immersion to increase the refractive index and bend light more sharply — revealing smaller details.

💡

Fiber Optics

Light bends back into the cable when it hits the wall at a shallow angle. Engineers calculate the exact angle for total internal reflection.

🌊

Mirages

Hot air has a lower refractive index. Light bends through temperature layers, creating the illusion of water on the road — the same law.
