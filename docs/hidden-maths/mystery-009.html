Discovery

The Hidden Formula

The fan does not just spin. It divides the circle into equal parts. Every part is a sector with a precise angle, arc, and area.

360° ÷ Blades = Sector Angle
360 ÷ 3 = 120°

Once you know the sector angle, one multiplication gives you the arc length and the exact pie slice of air each blade commands.

Explanation

How the Numbers Work

1

The Circle

The fan is a circle with 360° at the center. The blades are evenly spaced like spokes. The number of blades decides how many slices the circle is cut into.

360° ÷ n
2

The Sector Angle

Each blade sits in a sector. For 3 blades, the angle is 120°. For 5 blades, it is 72°. Smaller sectors mean more blades but less space for each to push air.

3 blades = 120°
3

The Arc Length

The curved path between two blade tips is an arc. Its length is a fraction of the full circumference. The formula is 2πr × (θ ÷ 360).

Arc = 2πr × θ/360
4

The Sector Area

Each blade commands a sector of air. The area is πr² × (θ ÷ 360). More blades = smaller sectors = smoother airflow but less power per blade.

Area = πr² × θ/360
Applications

Where Else Is the Sector Hiding?

Dividing a circle into equal sectors is one of geometry's oldest jobs. It appears everywhere rotation meets structure.

🌻

Flower Petals

Many flowers arrange seeds in sectors following the golden angle. The pattern is a spiral sector division — same geometry as the fan.

🍕

Pizza Slices

Cutting a pizza into 8 slices is exactly 360° ÷ 8 = 45° per sector. Every slice is a congruent sector — same math, same formula.

⚙️

Gears

Gear teeth are spaced in equal sectors around the pitch circle. The tooth spacing angle determines how smoothly the gear meshes.

🎡

Ferris Wheel

Each gondola is spaced at equal sector angles. The arc length between seats is calculated the same way as the fan blade spacing.

🕐

Clock Face

Each hour mark is 30° apart. The clock is a circle divided into 12 equal sectors — 360° ÷ 12 = 30°. Same formula as the fan.

🎯

Dartboard

A dartboard has 20 equal sectors. Each scoring region is 18° wide. The geometry is identical to a 20-blade fan.
